The City in Time  /  June 2018



The streets of Wachendorf are still. Empty. A single sedan emerges and bustles along the cobblestone road until the whirring of the motor disappears around the corner and silence is restored. The overwhelming landscape of green rustles briefly before leaning back in the warmth of the daylight. For some reason, my pace settles at a relaxed stroll. Is this place slower, or am I slower in it?

After weeks exploring cities in Northern Germany, this nine-hundred-person town suggests a different reality of time. Unlike the insomniac Berlin, Wachendorf rises and sets with the sun. Hours stretch to days, only to snap back in place with each glance down at my watch.

“We never really see time,” physicist Carlo Rovelli states, “We see only clocks. If you say this object moves, what you really mean is that this object is here when the hand of your clock is here, and so on. We say we measure time with clocks, but we see only the hands of the clocks, not time itself.”

Suppose we use Rovelli’s point to broaden our view of what we call “clock.” By this definition, any object with a predictable cycle of behavior acts as a clock. Naturally, the clock that comes to mind is a precise machine by which we measure time. However, a tree, too, is a clock — one of arguably more reliability and less precision than a wristwatch.

The clock we are most familiar with is the sun. From the passing of the sun overhead, we can record calendars of days, months, and years. But even this characterization can be misleading. Although the sun moves across our sky with incredible regularity, it is Earth’s planetary rotation and orbit around the sun that produce seasons, day, and night.

It is not a single object, then, whereby we can measure time, but at least two objects. The sun and Earth are a clock only as a pair, as with a watch’s hands and face. Their positions, relative to each other, determine our place in time. A tree is only a functioning clock insofar as it is supported with the regularity of the sun, the Earth, rain, and fertile soil. In fact, the closer we look, the larger these networks of objects grow into a single synchronized whole. The interdependence of nature’s cycles suggests the world itself possesses a certain circadian rhythm.

Sterile and flickering, a fluorescent tube reflects my eyes in an unidentified liquid pooled on the concrete platform. A rumble in the distance suddenly flashes across my face, bringing seconds of cool relief from the sticky air. A woman’s voice: “Einsteigen bitte.” Equal parts rowdy and drowsy, a small crowd pours into the train. In a corner seat, an older man is slumped over his backpack, the only indication that the city sleeps.

A train schedule is a clock of entirely different circumstances than the sun or a tree. Its cycles are driven by human behavior: patterns of traffic, arrival and departure locations, cost of operation. Synthetic clocks such as transit schedules are not coupled to each other like naturally occuring clocks are; they operate as independently as possible, sometimes in direct opposition to natural clocks. By doing this, they tell us a different story of time than what we might expect.

We have defined clocks as objects, or sets of objects, that have regular patterns of behavior. And, as Rovelli reminds us, we do not see time, but can only interpret it through these clocks. What happens to our sense of time, then, if we create an object (or set of objects) that changes our measurement of time? A Boeing 787 airplane can take off from London at 4:00PM and land in Chicago at 6:00PM, eight hours later. This seems odd. We can justify this discontinuity in our minds by sectioning off our world into different time zones. Nonetheless, the timeshift (read: jet lag) can be jarring.

An airplane, the afternoon rush-hour, the agitated chirping of a closing train door; these man-made clocks cannot operate in isolation. They constantly interact with the many natural clocks around them, and by doing so create a complex temporal landscape. In bustling areas of activity, does time accelerate with the increased tempo of comings and goings, pleases and thank yous, hellos and goodbyes? Perhaps the quiet neighborhoods slow down just so we can savor moments with our children before they grow up. Often somewhere in between the two, the city landscape is a patchwork of these fasts and slows.

Not long after we are returned to port, the water has receded from within the city’s warehouse district, exposing a thick green mud on the canal beds. Hamburgers continue about their business indifferently, like galoshes through a shallow puddle. Anticipating the tide and trade soon to come, the port eases to the gentle rise and fall of the waves. Upriver, a boy digs his toes deep in the sand.

In Hamburg, much of the waterfront infrastructure is tailored to the tide of the North Sea. The tide operates so regularly that the whole port follows a tide chart which dictates the volume of goods that can be sent up and down the Elbe. This water clock is supplanted with the regular cycles of arriving and departing vessels, dredging, and ferries. In contrast, the city itself is barricaded from the ebbs and flows of the water with a series of dikes and retaining walls that place the street high above the reach of the growing tides.

This dual nature of the Hamburg waterfront is addressed in a 2010 book titled Transforming Urban Waterfronts: Fixity and Flow. In it, authors Gene Desfor et al. posit that the urban landscape is “a variety of fixities (such as built environments, institutional and regulatory structures, and cultural practices) and flows (such as processes of capital accumulation, information, labor, finance capital, energy, and knowledge).” Their description of the city is a simpler model: objects moving through a field. But we know that the “fixities” they describe are not really static; buildings are built, renovated, altered, demolished; laws and cultures change. Even these fixities have predictable cycles of behavior and change, they just happen at slow enough rates to seem static in any given moment.

But if we are to revise Desfor et al.’s model to incorporate this constant change, we lose our footing. When nothing stays in place, how can we know where we are? Describing urban fixities is perhaps so attractive because it affords constants against which we can compare moments in time. If we deny the notion of fixities because their presence as static is momentary, we need a different constant by which we measure change. Perhaps change itself can fill that role.

We can take any rate of change, any clock with a rate of time, such as Earth and the sun, and create a calendar of cycles. The Earth rotates once on-axis in twenty-four hours, and completes this rotation 365.25 times in each revolution about the sun. On the surface of the Earth, this is our constant.

Similarly, if we consider a train that passes through a certain station every five minutes on weekdays and every ten minutes on the weekend, we can make a calendar of cycles. If we measure the cycles in minutes, we are using the Earth and the sun as our time reference. If we measure the cycles without an external constant, the cycles become our constant. When one cycle of the train coming is the measurement of time, time moves twice as fast on weekdays, or half as fast on weekends.

The implication is this: all clocks suggest a specific reality of time. These rates of time are determined by the length of a cycle of behavior. We can only consolidate these objects into the same timescape by choosing a single clock as constant (the Earth and sun), and measuring all other objects relative to it. Any traditional clock (the digital clock on your phone, for example) is simply a proxy for this constant, as hours, minutes, and seconds are derived from the twenty-four hour cycle. These clocks allow us to consolidate many timescales into a single, coherent worldview.

Chicago churns beneath a fog suspended. There is light but the sun is nowhere to be found. Watched from a window on the 23rd floor, toy cars flow through the streets. Towers, dozens of stories tall, stand unflinching, secretly swaying gently in the wind.

A residential highrise is topping out on the south side of Chicago. It will stand through the next generation, slated to be demolished decades later. For the architect, the project consumed four years of meetings, drawings, and construction. One of her many ongoing endeavors, it has sustained her practice in the meantime. Inside, rented apartments are distributed on annual leases. Maintenance crews check the fire safety building systems every month. In the lobby, the mail person delivers mail daily, the leasing agent shows spaces hourly, and the front door opens and closes at least once every thirty seconds.

Even within a single building, there are many overlapping timescales; they bring a place to life. How complex, then, is the city, with its networks of buildings, infrastructure, and landscapes? The challenge of the city occurs between these timescales; institutions and inhabitants find themselves in conflict often because of the dyssynchrony between their clocks.

In Amsterdam, for example, the city planning department is always responding to needs in the city. Bike lanes are too narrow and dangerous for their volume of traffic, housing is in great demand and short supply, and tourism strains the infrastructure of the city center. Even when the municipality owns and controls almost all the land in the city, the right solutions take time to be discussed, designed, and executed. Small changes can have significant delays, even where they are needed most. The city strains to accommodate.

Wheels and footsteps flood the narrow streets of Amsterdam. In the glow of a setting sun, bodies in motion scatter in every direction. I pedal hard against the cobblestone, frantically carving my path across bridges and tram tracks. Another cyclist rounds the corner and nearly clips my side — I inhale sharply: the smell of water. Slowing my pace, I continue to ride beneath the trees. The sun slowly disappears behind the roofline and the rush subsides with it.

How do we navigate the city in dynamic time? It is perhaps comfortable to rely on the predictable rhythm of the sun, but by doing so we cull the richness of the city’s temporal landscape. Every city on Earth, composed of a unique set of bodies, experiences different rates of time. What makes the day if the summer sun never sets in Alaska?

The architecture and construction of time is an effect of the world we experience — a world we continue to build for ourselves. Our inventions adhere to the solar clock, distort it, sometimes ignore its existence. Cities, as singularities of man-made bodies, create new temporal landscapes to inhabit: landscapes for us to live, to dream, to remember.

A Lithuanian woman motions for me to stand so she can access the miniature airplane bathroom. I duck my head into the aisle and look forward at one-hundred people in one-hundred seats watching one-hundred movies on one-hundred tiny screens. The cabin glows purple. The windows sheath the daylight just enough to distort my sense of sunlight, and a pocket of low air pressure rocks the plane gently. In this moment, with no Earth, with no Sun, time threatens to stand still.
